01 — Why I'm Interested

This Doesn't Feel Like
a Blank-Slate Idea.

What stuck with me after meeting you is that KissCam does not feel like something that needs to be invented. It feels like an asset with history, recognition, public signals and multiple possible commercial lanes. The opportunity, in my read, is not to start from scratch. It is to clarify what already exists, package it better and make the commercial path easier for partners to see.

01 —
You Already Have Cultural Recognition
The Kiss Cam is a 35-year-old crowd ritual recognized in arenas and broadcast sports worldwide. That recognition is not something a funded startup can buy or replicate quickly. It already belongs to the moment . I believe it belongs to you.
02 —
The Moment Is Emotionally Instant
When I talk about KissCam to someone, they smile immediately. That kind of instant emotional recognition is extraordinarily rare in commercial products. Sponsors pay large sums to attach their brand to exactly this type of moment — shared, positive, high-attention and universal.
03 —
Sponsors Can Understand the Feeling Quickly
Most sponsorship categories require long explanations. The Kiss Cam does not. "Your brand, on screen, at the moment the whole crowd is watching and smiling." That pitch writes itself. The commercial story is already there. It just needs cleaner packaging around it.
04 —
Venues Already Know the Behavior
No venue entertainment director needs to be educated about what a Kiss Cam is. That removes a major friction point in the sales process. What they may need is a structured offer they can budget, approve and deploy. That is exactly what I think we could build together.
05 —
The Commercial Story Can Be Made Clearer
Right now, from what I observed publicly, the commercial story is scattered across different pages and audiences. I don't think that reflects the actual opportunity. I think one focused, buyer-ready web presence could significantly change how partners perceive and engage with KissCam.
⚠ —
A Note on My Read
Everything I observed is based on publicly available signals and our conversation. I don't know the full picture. That's why I want to sit down with you. Some of what I found may be outdated, superseded or more nuanced than what's visible from the outside. I'd love to hear where I got it right and where I missed.
02 — What I Found After Digging In

Valuable Pieces
Already on the Board.

After our conversation I went deeper, reviewing the public website, the way the commercial story is currently presented and where the gaps in the buyer path seem to be. My overall read: there are real assets here. The opportunity is in how they're organized and packaged, not in whether the underlying value exists.

What I Noticed
The Site Leans Fan-Facing
The current homepage speaks warmly to fans — which makes sense given the brand's cultural identity. But a venue director or sponsorship buyer landing there today may not immediately see a clear path to a conversation. Those are two different audiences, and they respond to different signals.
What's Already There
The Commercial Signals Are Real
What stood out to me is how much commercial activity is already visible — contests, promotions, licensing language, patent references, the AR Mirror, the photo booth experience, ESTV, Virtue Beauty, The Brand Liaison. These are not small signals. They suggest real deals and real products in various stages.
What Could Be Clearer
The Buyer Path Needs a Direct Lane
When I put myself in the shoes of a venue entertainment director or brand activation manager, I found it hard to identify a clean next step. I think a structured buyer path, with specific pages for venues, sponsors and licensing partners, could make a meaningful difference in how many of the right conversations KissCam starts.
What Could Be Clearer
Venue and Sponsor Audiences Need Their Own Lane
A venue buyer and a sponsor buyer have different questions, different budgets and different approval processes. Right now they appear to be served by the same general pages. Separating them, with specific value propositions and dedicated inquiry flows, is one of the clearest packaging wins I see.
What Could Be Clearer
Licensing and Media Need a Packaging Layer
There appear to be real licensing and media relationships already in place. But from the outside, it's hard to understand what's available, how to access it and what the inquiry process looks like. A clear licensing overview, even a basic one, would make those conversations easier to start.
What Could Be Clearer
Proof Could Work Harder
I didn't find structured case studies or named deployment stories on the current site. I understand those may be confidential or still in development. But even a framework that signals "here's the kind of result this creates" would help buyers build confidence faster. Proof is what closes deals.
Where I'd Focus
The Inquiry Flow Could Be a Competitive Advantage
Most fan engagement and event activation companies make it genuinely hard to buy. A frictionless inquiry flow built specifically for venues, sponsors and licensing partners could become one of KissCam's strongest conversion tools. It's an undervalued asset in this category.
Where I'd Focus
The Visual System Could Feel More Premium
The underlying brand (a heart, a kiss, a crowd moment) is inherently premium-emotional. I think the visual presentation of the commercial side of KissCam could more confidently reflect that. See the Mockups section for what I have in mind.

03 — The Landscape

This Is the Part That
Made Me More Excited.

KissCam sits at the intersection of several markets that already understand attention, emotion and live moments. That is actually a significant advantage. The buyers in these categories have budgets, established procurement processes and a vocabulary for what they want. You do not have to create demand. You have to show up in a way they recognize.

01
Venue Entertainment
Jumbotron moments, in-game prompts, fan cams, proposal nights, theme nights and crowd participation are established parts of the live-event playbook. Venues already invest in these activations because they drive dwell time, concession revenue and renewal sentiment. KissCam fits naturally into that existing budget category.
02
Sponsor Activation
Brands want emotional, shareable moments that attach their name to positive attention at the peak of audience focus. In-venue Kiss Cam segments are among the most organically shared moments in sports. That organic distribution, when packaged as a sponsor vehicle, is a compelling commercial argument.
03
Fan Engagement Technology
Multiple funded companies compete in fan engagement tech: mobile submissions, AR tools, screen integrations, photo experiences. Here is what they cannot buy: a named, 35-year-old crowd ritual with organic recognition. That is KissCam's moat. The technology is increasingly commoditized. The brand is not.
04
Short-Form Media & Content
Kiss Cam moments generate organic social distribution at scale. When rights are clear and packaging is structured, that content reach can become a legitimate media and licensing asset. I think there is a meaningful conversation to be had here. But it requires a cleaner rights and content framework first.
05
Licensing & Brand Extension
The emotional territory of the Kiss Cam (romantic moments, crowd joy, connection) extends naturally into consumer products, Valentine's campaigns, date-night experiences, beauty, gifts and event-based activations. These are real licensing categories. If the IP is clear, that conversation is worth having.
06
Event Agencies & Promoters
Agencies and promoters need packaged concepts they can pitch to clients, budget and activate without extensive custom development. A venue-ready KissCam package with clear deliverables, activation specs and commercial terms removes friction and opens a distribution channel that does not require direct venue relationships.
04 — The Window

Why I Think
the Timing Matters.

I don't want to overstate this as urgency. The broader market around fan engagement, short-form content, sponsorship activation and event technology is moving fast. Adjacent players are packaging similar emotional territory. There is still meaningful white space for KissCam to own. Packaging it sooner creates a clearer head start.

Who KissCam Actually Competes With for Venue and Sponsor Budgets
  • Fan Engagement Platforms
    CUE Uplause Monterosa Fan of the Match
    Well-funded, actively pitching venues and sponsors. Strong on tech and data. No consumer recognition, no emotional identity, no named cultural moment. KissCam has all three.
  • Stadium AR & Live Event Tech
    ARound by Stagwell
    The most direct tech competitor. Deployed in NFL and MLB stadiums. Competing for the same venue entertainment budget. Strong on execution, weak on emotional narrative. No brand fans actually know by name.
  • Who KissCam Should Be Selling To
    Brands like Bumble, Hinge, Zola and Match spend heavily to attach themselves to romantic public moments. KissCam creates exactly those moments. They are not competitors — they are target sponsors. The same is true for jewelry brands, flower delivery services and Valentine's-adjacent advertisers.

Two Competitors I Researched in Depth
CUE — connectwithcue.com
Fan Engagement Platform
CUE is a well-funded fan engagement software company with real venue deployments and a clean B2B interface. But my full analysis found a critical clarity problem: too many products with too many names targeting too many audiences. A venue buyer landing on their site has to work to understand what CUE actually sells. They have built a strong tool suite with a weak commercial story. That is the exact inverse of what KissCam needs to be.
→  Strategic read: CUE has infrastructure but no cultural moment. KissCam has the moment and needs the commercial structure. These are not the same problem.
ARound — aroundar.com
Shared AR Platform — Backed by Stagwell / NASDAQ: STGW
ARound is backed by a $2B+ publicly traded company (Stagwell) with real deployments at the LA Rams, Minnesota Twins (25+ minute average engagement), Cleveland Cavaliers, Kansas City Royals and Minnesota United. They have broadcast AR and mobile AR synced in real time. Serious technology. Serious money. And one clear gap: zero consumer brand recognition. Fans do not know ARound exists. They experience it and forget it.
→  Strategic read: They built the pipe. KissCam owns the content. That is a meaningful distinction . It is a conversation worth having before ARound decides to build a brand around the same emotional territory.
